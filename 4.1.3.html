Wi-Fi and Bluetooth

How computers use Wi-Fi and bluetooth to connect to network

Wi-Fi

A wireless transmitter (WAP) receives information from a network, it's connection (for example a broadband connection if the internet is used). This transmitter converts the received information into the radio waves and then transmits them. A device for example a computer, recives the radio waves from an installed wireless adaptor which allows it to download the information from the data source. This works in reverse when the device wants to transmit data over the network. Wi-Fi is best suited to operate full-scale network because it offers a much faster data trasfer rate.

Bluetooth

Bluetooth sends and recives radio waves in a band of 79 different frequencies (also known as channels). They are all centred on a 2.45 GHz frequency. They don't interfere with other fecices since each communicating pair uses a different channel (from the 79 options). When a device wants to communicate, it picks one of the 79 channels randomly. If the channel is being used already, It then randomly picks another channel. This is known as spread-spectrum frequency hopping. To lower the risk of interfering with other devices, the communication pairs constantly change the frequencies they are using.
